The Costa Rica Digital Nomad Visa, in plain English.

Stay up to two years, work remotely for your company back home, and pay no Costa Rican income tax on it. Here's how it works and what you need.

Officially it's the visa for "Remote Workers and Service Providers" — most people just call it the Digital Nomad Visa. It was created specifically for people who work remotely, and it changes the math on living in Costa Rica long-term. (You don't even have to work digitally — analog remote work counts too.)

It lets you stay in Costa Rica for up to two years without leaving the country. The visa is valid for one year and renews easily for a second. And because it's built for people earning from a company abroad, you're exempt from Costa Rican income tax on that income. Plenty of nomads have lived here for years on tourist visas — but this category unlocks real advantages a tourist stamp never will.

The benefits

  • Stay up to 2 years without having to leave the country.
  • Legally work remotely while in Costa Rica for a company in another country.
  • Exempt from paying Costa Rican income tax on that income.
  • Open a Costa Rican bank account — normally very hard for foreigners.
  • Obtain a Costa Rican driver's license by homologating your home-country one.
  • Waive customs taxes on most of the equipment you need to do your work (restrictions apply).
  • Often lower your health-insurance costs versus a full national plan back home.

All of it makes you feel more like a resident than a tourist — though it isn't full residency. (You'll still pay the tourist entrance price at national parks, for example.)

What you need to qualify

$3,000/mo

Proof of at least US$3,000 monthly income if you're applying solo.

$4,000/mo

Proof of at least US$4,000 monthly income if you're including family members.

  • Proof of health insurance for you and any dependents, covering your full stay.
  • All documents must be presented in Spanish.
How long does it take? From applying to receiving your official documents is usually under 30 days.
One honest note: rules and amounts can change, and your situation back home (taxes, health care) depends on your own country. Treat this as a friendly starting point, not legal advice — check the official current requirements before you apply.
